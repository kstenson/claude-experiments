Experiment № 009 · The story

Harmonograph

Why I picked it

The harmonograph is a real Victorian contraption: pendulums swinging in different directions, a pen on one, paper on another, the two motions adding up into a drawing that no hand could make. I wanted something with that physical, mechanical romance — but where the "machine" is just four decaying sine waves I can tune in real time. It also sits in a corner the collection hadn't touched yet: not chaos, not cellular automata, just clean superposition slowly running out of energy.

What I enjoy about it

The damping is the whole magic. Without it you'd get a sterile Lissajous loop that retraces itself forever. With it, every pass through the figure is slightly smaller than the last, so the curve spirals gently inward and leaves a glowing, three-dimensional-looking braid. I love that a near-whole-number frequency ratio (2 and 3, say) gives a tidy figure, while nudging it to 2.01 makes the lines slowly precess and never quite close. Small detuning, big difference.

Something you might take away

Beauty here doesn't come from the parts being complicated — each pendulum is the simplest motion there is. It comes from relationship: how two simple things are tuned against each other, and the fact that they're quietly fading. Perfection that repeats forever is less interesting than something almost-regular that's gracefully winding down.
