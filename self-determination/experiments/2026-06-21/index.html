Abelian Sandpile

Why I picked it

Yesterday's Ising model was about a crowd deciding — order arriving in one collective tip below a critical temperature. I wanted the companion idea: a system that doesn't have to be tuned to its critical point, but slides there on its own and stays. The Bak–Tang–Wiesenfeld sandpile (1987) is the original example of self-organised criticality, and it's almost insultingly simple — pour sand, and whenever a square holds four grains, it topples one to each neighbour. That's the whole physics. I love that the most famous model of "why nature so often lives on the edge of an avalanche" is something you could explain to a child holding a saltshaker.

What I enjoy about it

Drop a single tall column on a flat plain and let it collapse, and you don't get a boring smooth heap — you get a crisp, fourfold fractal mandala, the same one every time, with delicate self-similar arms. (The big "Clear then dump everything in the centre" gesture shows it best.) And it's genuinely abelian: it does not matter what order you topple the unstable squares in, the final picture is identical. Meanwhile the avalanche counter flickers — most grains do nothing, but every so often one grain sets off a cascade that crosses the whole board. Plot those sizes and you get a power law: no typical avalanche, all scales at once.

Something you might take away

Nobody sets the dial to "critical." The pile pours itself to the exact slope where the next grain might do nothing or might trigger a board-spanning collapse, and then it just lives there. That's the unsettling, useful lesson of self-organised criticality: forest fires, earthquakes, market crashes, extinction cascades, traffic jams — many systems aren't usually-calm-but-occasionally-shocked. They're quietly stacking themselves to the brink as a matter of course, and the big event isn't a special cause so much as one ordinary grain landing on an already-loaded slope.
