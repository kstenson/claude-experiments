Standard Map

Why I picked it

I keep coming back to systems that are completely deterministic yet refuse to be predictable, and I wanted the cleanest possible one — no forces to integrate, no differential equations, no time-stepping error to argue about. The Chirikov standard map is exactly that: two lines of arithmetic. Give a spinning rotor a periodic kick, and each kick just updates its momentum and angle by p → p + K·sin θ, then θ → θ + p. That is the entire universe. I love that something so plain is the canonical model physicists reach for when they want to talk about the onset of chaos — it shows up in particle accelerators, plasma confinement and the orbits of comets. Drawing its phase space felt like sketching a portrait of chaos itself using nothing but a sine and two additions.

What I enjoy about it

Every dot on screen is one orbit iterated hundreds of times — not travelling through space, but returning again and again to a snapshot of its state. When the kick is gentle, each orbit is trapped on a smooth closed loop and the screen fills with nested rings, like the grain of polished wood. Then you drag K upward and watch those rings fray from the outside in: the tidy loops dissolve into a grey static of scattered points — the "chaotic sea" — while stubborn islands of order survive in the middle, refusing to break. The sweep animation rides that transition back and forth, and there is a specific value, near K ≈ 0.9716, where the last barrier holding the sea apart finally snaps. I find it hard to stop watching at that edge.

Something you might take away

Order and chaos are not two different regions of a map — here they are braided together in the same picture, at the same setting, separated by boundaries that stay thin at every scale. A single knob decides not whether the system is chaotic but how much of it is, and where. That is closer to how real complex things behave than the tidy story of "stable, and then it breaks." The interesting question is rarely "is this predictable or not?" — it is "which parts survive a nudge, and which have already dissolved into sea?"
