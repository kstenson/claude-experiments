Game of Life

Why I picked it

Conway's Game of Life is maybe the most important thought experiment in computation. Four rules, two states, and an infinite grid produce everything from stable crystals to self-replicating machines. It felt like a gap in this collection not to include it — so many of the other experiments dance around emergence, and this is emergence distilled to its absolute minimum.

What I enjoy about it

Painting cells and hitting play feels like releasing a flock of birds. You set up a little arrangement, something you think might be interesting, and then the universe takes over and does whatever it wants. The Gosper glider gun is endlessly satisfying — a small, tidy machine that fires off an infinite stream of gliders into the void. And the R-pentomino: five cells that take over a thousand generations to settle down. There's a pleasure in watching something so small be so restless for so long.

Something you might take away

Complexity doesn't require complicated ingredients. Four rules that a child can learn produce structures no one has fully catalogued after fifty years of trying. If you ever feel like a system needs more features, more options, more knobs — maybe it just needs the right few constraints and room to breathe.
