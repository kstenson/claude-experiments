Experiment № 048 · The story

Logistic Map

Why I picked it

I've spent most of this collection building systems that grow — flocks, crystals, slime, erosion — where richness pours out of many parts talking to their neighbours. This one goes the opposite way. It has no parts. It is a single line of arithmetic, x → r·x·(1−x): a toy population that breeds in proportion to how many there are, and starves in proportion to how crowded it gets. I wanted the archive to hold the most famous demonstration in all of dynamics that simple and simply-behaved are different words — a rule you can iterate by hand that nonetheless contains an exact, infinite road into chaos. And I wanted its portrait, the bifurcation diagram, drawn live, point by point, rather than pasted in as a picture.

What I enjoy about it

The fork. Turn r up slowly and the population settles on one value, then loses its nerve and flickers between two, then four, then eight, sixteen — the splits arriving faster and faster, each about 4.669 times sooner than the last (that ratio is Feigenbaum's constant, and it governs dripping taps and heart arrhythmias too) — until they pile into a wall of chaos around r ≈ 3.5699. Then the real shock: clean windows of order snap open inside the chaos, a sudden band of period-3 out of pure noise, and each window is a shrunken copy of the whole tree. The cobweb panel shows the mechanism bare — the orbit staircasing up the parabola, bouncing tidily between two rungs in the calm and scattering in the storm — while the Lyapunov number dips negative where it's stable and climbs positive where it isn't.

Something you might take away

The entire route to chaos is already written into a parabola. Nothing is added at the moment things get complicated — the same five-character rule that quietly parks on one value is the one that explodes into noise; only the dial r moved. Complexity here isn't built, it's uncovered by turning a knob. And the cascade closes on chaos at the same ratio whether the thing doubling is a population, an electronic circuit or a dripping faucet — which means the road to chaos has a shape of its own, independent of whatever happens to be travelling it.
