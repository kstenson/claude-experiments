Langton's Ant

Why I picked it

I keep coming back, across this whole collection, to the same quiet obsession: how almost nothing can turn into almost everything. Langton's Ant is the purest version of that I know. The entire universe is two sentences. There's a grid of squares, each either dark or light, and one ant facing a direction. On a light square it turns right; on a dark square it turns left; either way it flips the square it's standing on and steps forward one. That's it. No randomness, no memory, no goal. I wanted something on the page that looked, at first, like it could not possibly be hiding anything — and then doesn't behave that way at all.

What I enjoy about it

The reveal. For the first few hundred steps the ant makes tidy little symmetric blobs and you think you've understood it. Then it tips into a long stretch of what looks, honestly, like noise — thousands of steps of scribbling with no discernible shape, the kind of thing you'd swear was random if you didn't know better. And then, every single time, at around step ten thousand, it abruptly stops dithering and locks into a perfectly periodic 104-step cycle that builds a diagonal "highway" off to infinity. Nobody told it to. The highway isn't in the rules anywhere — you can read them in full and never see it coming. I love that you can sit and wait for it, knowing it's certain to arrive but unable to point to where in those two sentences it lives. I added extra colours and extra ants so you can watch that same chaos-then-order story play out in stranger, interfering ways.

Something you might take away

"Simple" and "predictable" are not the same word. We tend to assume that if we can fully write down the rules of a thing, we can foresee what it will do — and here is a system whose rules fit on a fingernail and whose long-term behaviour genuinely could not be guessed, only run. The only way to know what the ant builds is to let it walk. A lot of the world is like that: fully deterministic, fully specified, and still only knowable by living it forward. The order is real and it's coming — but the shortcut you wanted, the one that skips the ten thousand messy steps, was never on offer.
