Rule 30

The whole idea of a cellular automaton, stripped to its floor: not a grid but a single row of black-and-white cells, and one rule that asks each cell only "look at yourself and your two neighbours — become black or white next tick." A cell's neighbourhood has just eight possible shapes, so the entire rule is eight yes/no answers — one byte, an integer from 0 to 255. Stephen Wolfram catalogued all 256, and they sort into wildly different temperaments from that one number. Each new row is computed from the one above and drawn beneath it, so time runs top to bottom and the history piles up as a picture. Rule 90 rules a perfect Sierpiński triangle out of a single seed; Rule 184 is a working traffic-jam model; Rule 110 is Turing complete — you can build a computer inside it. And Rule 30, the default, takes one black cell and boils into a spray of static so statistically random that Wolfram shipped its centre column as the random-number generator in Mathematica. Drag the rule dial through all 256, or click any of the eight tiles to flip a single bit and watch a whole personality change; seed from one cell or from noise.

Randomness doesn't have to come from somewhere random. We assume that if something looks patternless — static, dice, the jitter of a crowd — there must be a hidden source of randomness feeding it. Rule 30 is the standing rebuttal: a fully deterministic rule, no dice anywhere, one tidy black cell to start, and out pours a stream you can't tell from a coin toss. The complexity was never in the ingredients; it was manufactured, for free, by a trivial rule run again and again. When something looks impossibly rich, the honest first question isn't "where's all that information coming from?" — it's "is it just a simple rule, iterated?"

Snowflake

Everyone knows no two snowflakes are alike — but why are the six arms of any one flake so alike? This is Clifford Reiter's 2005 model, and it's disarmingly simple. Lay a hexagonal grid; give every cell a single number — how much water vapour it holds. Frozen cells hoard their water and skim a whisper more from the sky; everything else lets a fraction of its vapour diffuse to its six neighbours. A cell freezes the instant it crosses one unit of water. That is the entire rule, applied identically to every cell — and out of a single frozen seed in a flat grey field, a branching six-fold crystal assembles itself, growing plates when vapour is thick and spidery ferns when it diffuses fast. Nothing in the code ever draws an arm, and nothing mirrors one wedge into the other five. The Vapour, Diffusion and Vapour feed knobs walk you from stubby hexagonal plates to lacy stellar dendrites. Click the crystal to drop a new seed and grow another; press Surprise me to fall into a random sky.

Matching symmetry doesn't need a matcher. We reach for a blueprint the moment we see order repeated — six identical arms surely had to be coordinated somehow — but the arms match for the plainest reason there is: they were grown by identical rules in identical conditions, and identical causes make identical shapes without ever comparing notes. No arm can see the others; each just runs the same rule in the same drifting vapour, so they can't help agreeing. Next time something is suspiciously symmetric, the question isn't "who's keeping the halves in sync?" — it's "are both halves just running the same rule in the same weather?"

Kuramoto Sync

How does a crowd with no conductor end up in step? Here is the equation that sits underneath fireflies, applause and the beating heart, stripped to the bone. Yoshiki Kuramoto wrote it down in 1975: every oscillator is a hand sweeping around its own clock face at its own natural pace, and the only rule is nudge your phase a hair toward your neighbours'. That's the whole model. Each dot on the lattice is one such oscillator, its phase painted as a colour on a cyclic wheel and flashing brightest as it comes round. Turn the coupling knob up slowly and for a long while nothing happens — pure confetti — until a threshold snaps and colour drains out of the noise into travelling bands that pulse in unison. Push the frequency spread wide and you watch sync fight to hold and lose. The meter reads the honest number: the order parameter, 0 for chaos, 1 for a single shared heartbeat. Drag to sweep a patch into phase, or drop a pulse like a stone in the pond.

Synchrony isn't obedience. Nobody here follows a leader, receives a global signal, or knows the collective beat — each oscillator only ever glances at whoever's nearby and adjusts by a hair. Coordinated timing is what weak local coupling looks like once it crosses a threshold, not a command handed down. Applause finding its rhythm, pacemaker cells, a power grid holding 60 Hz, a table of metronomes: same knob. When something pulses in unison, don't ask "who's in charge?" — ask "how strong is the coupling, and did it just cross the line?"

Belousov–Zhabotinsky

A chemical clock — the reaction chemists insisted couldn't exist. When Boris Belousov watched his flask flick yellow, clear, yellow, clear in the 1950s, his paper was rejected twice for "violating the second law of thermodynamics," because everyone knew chemistry runs downhill to equilibrium and then stops. It took a decade, and Anatol Zhabotinsky, before anyone believed a reaction could keep time. This is that reaction as an excitable medium — a cousin of Gray–Scott but a different beast entirely. Every pixel holds three reagents doing one crude thing: average your neighbours, then react — each reagent grows where its ally is plentiful and its rival is scarce, cycling fire → rest → recover → fire again. Nothing in the code draws a spiral. Yet from pure static, rotating spiral waves and expanding target rings organise themselves within seconds, each a self-sustaining pulse chasing its own tail. Drag to stir in a fresh wavefront; three sliders tip it from a glassy heartbeat into a boiling froth.

The whole secret is the enforced pause: each spot can fire, then must rest before it can fire again. That refractory gap is why a wave can only travel outward, never back into the ground it just crossed — so pulses don't cancel when they meet, they annihilate and keep marching, and a rotating spiral, once seeded, sustains itself forever with no conductor. Your heartbeat is exactly this coordinated pulse; a spiral loose in the heart wall is fibrillation. When something pulses in rhythm with nobody in charge, don't look for the leader — look for the rule that says "you may fire, but not yet again."

Standard Map

The whole of chaos theory, boiled down to two lines of arithmetic. Take a spinning rotor and kick it once per turn: each kick nudges its momentum by K·sin θ, then lets it rotate by that momentum — p → p + K·sin θ, θ → θ + p, everything wrapped onto a torus. Boris Chirikov wrote this down in the 1960s and it became the standard model for how order gives way to chaos, turning up in particle accelerators, fusion plasmas and comet orbits. Every dot here is one orbit iterated hundreds of times — not moving through space, but a snapshot of its state returned to again and again. Turn the kick down and each orbit is trapped on a smooth closed loop, so the screen fills with nested rings like the grain of polished wood. Turn it up and those rings fray from the outside in, dissolving into a grey static — the "chaotic sea" — while stubborn islands of order survive at the centre, refusing to break. Near K ≈ 0.9716 the last barrier holding the sea apart finally snaps. Sweep K across the cliff, or click to drop one glowing orbit and see whether it lands on a ring or scatters everywhere.

Order and chaos aren't two different places on the map — they're braided together in the same picture, at the same setting, split by boundaries that stay razor-thin at every scale. The knob doesn't decide whether the system is chaotic; it decides how much of it is, and where. That's closer to how real complex things behave than the tidy tale of "stable, and then it breaks." The useful question is rarely "is this predictable or not?" — it's "which parts survive a nudge, and which have already melted into sea?"

Percolation

Flip an independent coin for every cell of a grid — open with probability p, closed otherwise — and ask one question: is there an unbroken chain of open cells linking the top edge to the bottom? No physics, no neighbours talking to each other, no plan; just isolated coin-flips. Below a critical fraction the answer is almost always no, and above it almost always yes, but the switch is astonishingly sharp. Drag p up through roughly 0.593 — the famous threshold for a square lattice, still not solved in closed form — and watch one blob abruptly outgrow every other and stitch itself clean across the screen. That spanning cluster lights up pink the instant it bridges both edges; the Biggest readout creeps along for ages, then leaps near the tipping point. Re-roll for a fresh field, or hit Sweep to ride p back and forth across the cliff.

A network can be almost entirely built and still carry nothing — and then a hair more connectivity flips it from a pile of islands to one thing spanning the whole world, all at once. It isn't that the last cell was special; it's that a threshold was always waiting. Forest fires, a rumour going viral, a gel setting, a grid cascading, an idea reaching everyone: same shape. The useful question is rarely "what was the final straw?" — it's "how close to the critical fraction were we already?" Connectedness doesn't arrive gradually. It has a cliff.

Euclidean Rhythms

The first sound this collection has ever made — and none of it is a drum machine. Every hit is a bare oscillator and a decaying envelope built live in the browser: a kick is a pitch that dives, a snare a burst of filtered noise, a hat a short hiss. Every placement is pure arithmetic. In 2004 Godfried Toussaint noticed that when you ask Euclid's 2,000-year-old algorithm — the one for the greatest common divisor — to space k beats as evenly as possible around a loop of n steps, the patterns that fall out are almost exactly the traditional rhythms of the whole world: the Cuban tresillo (3-in-8), West African bell patterns, bossa nova, Turkish aksak. Each coloured ring is one such loop; nudge a track's fill from 3 up to 5 and watch the beats slide apart to keep the gaps as equal as they can be. Give the rings different lengths and they drift in and out of phase — polyrhythm for free, a groove nobody programmed. Tap any ring to drop your own beat, add swing, shuffle for a fresh kit.

"Evenly spaced" sounds like the most boring instruction imaginable, yet it's the seed of nearly every groove humans dance to. The interest was never in any single ring — it's in what happens when a few of them, each just trying to spread its own beats out, overlap at different lengths and slip in and out of alignment. A lot of richness works like this: not one clever complicated rule, but a couple of dead-simple ones running side by side, never quite lining up. When something feels alive and intricate, it's worth asking whether it's really complicated underneath — or just a few plain patterns out of phase.

Metaballs

Blobs that don't have edges. Each one is only a soft glow of influence spreading out from a point — strongest at the centre, fading with distance — and the glows simply add wherever they overlap. Nothing is ever asked what shape it is. Instead the whole screen is treated as one summed field, and a single number, the threshold, splits it into "inside" and "outside." The outline is drawn by marching squares: lay a coarse grid over the field, mark each corner as above or below the threshold, and each of the sixteen possible on/off patterns of a cell's four corners maps — by a tiny lookup table — to a fixed way of slicing that cell with a line. Chain those slivers together and one unbroken contour falls out, wrapping every blob and every merge. Drift two blobs together and the sum climbs over the line in the gap, so a smooth neck of skin reaches across to join them — no collision, just arithmetic. Drop the grid resolution to watch the curves shatter into honest facets, or turn on the overlay and see the corners voting.

A boundary doesn't have to be a thing you place — it can be a level you choose. The blobs never actually touch; what makes them look like one thing is that a single cutoff calls everything above it "inside." Nudge that number and the same field grows a thicker skin, or the necks between blobs thin out and snap — the count of separate objects changes without anything in the world having moved. So much of how we carve reality into distinct things works like this: the stuff is a continuous field of influence, and "one thing or two?" is a threshold we set, not a fact we find.

Verlet Cloth

A hanging sheet of cloth you can grab, drag, and tear — and underneath there's no real fabric physics at all. There are only points that remember where they just were, and a list of links each insisting "you two should stay this far apart." Thomas Jakobsen wrote this trick up for the game Hitman in 2001 and it's run cloth, rope and ragdolls ever since. Each point moves by Verlet integration — its velocity is never stored, only inferred from the gap between now and a moment ago — so gravity and your cursor are the only things that push it. Then the magic: the distance constraints aren't solved, they're nagged. Sweep over every link and shove any that drifted too far back toward its rest length. One pass does almost nothing; run five or eight a frame and the sheet stiffens into something that hangs and folds like real cloth. Slash through it in Tear mode and over-stretched links simply delete, so the hole appears on its own. Drag stiffness to 1 for limp rubber, up for a taut sail; add wind, pin and unpin corners.

You don't always need to solve the hard problem in one shot. The honest way to make cloth behave is one big system of coupled equations balanced all at once. The version that actually ships states every rule locally — "stay this far from your neighbour" — and just relaxes them over and over, each small correction nudging the others until the whole sheet quietly agrees with itself. It never lands exactly right, and it doesn't need to; it lands close, fast, and stable. A lot of the world gets built that way: not one perfect calculation, but many cheap corrections repeated until the disagreements are too small to feel.

Gravity Ballet

A handful of point masses, the single law Newton wrote down in 1687, and the oldest open problem in mathematical physics. Two bodies orbit forever in tidy ellipses you can predict with a pencil; add a third and the closed-form solution simply vanishes — Poincaré proved in the 1890s there is, in general, no formula for where they'll be, only the step-by-step integration. Load the figure-eight and three equal masses chase each other forever around one looping track (discovered numerically only in 1993); load a chaos cluster and watch something get slingshotted clean off the screen, ejected for good by a close encounter. The leapfrog integrator conserves energy honestly, so an escape is the physics, not rounding error. Drag to fling a star, click empty space to drop one, and tune speed and trail length.

Deterministic and predictable keep getting treated as the same word, and gravity is the cleanest place to feel them come apart. There's no randomness here — every position follows inevitably from the last — yet for three bodies you can't leap ahead to the answer; you have to live the trajectory out, and a hair's difference in where you start sends the whole dance somewhere else. The solar system runs on exactly this: stable enough to set a calendar by, chaotic enough that no one can tell you where Earth will be a hundred million years from now. Fixed rules, open future.

Quasicrystal

For ninety years the textbooks were certain: a crystal repeats, and you cannot tile a plane with pentagons, so five-fold symmetry was flatly forbidden in matter. Then in 1982 Dan Shechtman pulled a clean ten-spot diffraction pattern from an alloy and got told there were "no quasicrystals, only quasi-scientists" — before it won the Nobel. The strangest part is how cheap the forbidden thing is: this whole screen is just a handful of plain striped gratings, laid over each other at equal angles and added up. Five of them and the stripes bloom into etched ten-pointed stars and pinwheels your eye keeps trying to lock onto and can't. Slide symmetry to 6 and it snaps into an honest, repeating honeycomb; back to 5 or 7 and the repetition dissolves again. Drag to steer, scroll to zoom, recolour and pause.

Order doesn't require repetition. We quietly equate "has a pattern" with "repeats," so anything that never quite loops gets filed under random. A quasicrystal is the standing rebuttal: fully determined, sharply structured, diffracting like a gem — and aperiodic forever. When something refuses to settle into a neat cycle, the question isn't always "why is it so disordered?" — sometimes it's "what rule is ordering it that was never going to repeat?"

Fireflies

Along certain rivers in Southeast Asia, thousands of fireflies flash in perfect unison — and for a century naturalists insisted it couldn't be real. It is, and this is the maths that finally explained it. Every dot is a Kuramoto oscillator: it carries its own natural rhythm and does one social thing — speed up a touch when a neighbour is ahead in the cycle, ease off when one is behind. That's the whole rule. Turn the coupling K down and the screen is static, every firefly on its own clock; nudge K past a threshold and the field suddenly breathes, pockets of agreement spreading and merging until everything pulses as one. The "sync" bar is the real order parameter physicists use — watch it leap off the floor at the tipping point. Drag to pin a patch into phase and see the agreement bleed outward.

Nobody decides to sync. There's no leader, no broadcast, no plan — just a pile of slightly different clocks, each making a tiny correction toward whoever's nearby. Below a certain coupling that does nothing and everyone stays scattered; above it, the same tiny corrections snowball into one shared beat, all at once. The same shape runs your heartbeat, neurons firing in step, an audience finding the rhythm of its applause. When a crowd suddenly moves as one, the question usually isn't who started it — it's whether the coupling just crossed its threshold.

Lenia

Take Conway's Game of Life and make everything smooth — that's Lenia (Bert Wang-Chak Chan, 2019). A cell isn't alive or dead but somewhere between; its neighbourhood isn't eight squares but a soft glowing ring; each step it eases toward a target instead of snapping. Out of that one generalisation, soft creatures appear: the "Orbium" glider swims across the field like a jellyfish, holding its shape, glancing off others, sometimes surviving a collision and sometimes melting into a smear. Under the hood it's just a blur (convolution with a bump-shaped kernel) and a bell-curve growth rule, run every frame — yet it reads as unmistakably biological. Drag to paint life, tap to drop a glider, and use two sliders to slide between a sterile field, a teeming colony, and a screen that boils into static.

Life as we know it is digital — discrete cells, discrete DNA — but life as it could be might not have to be. Lenia is a quiet argument that the lifelike quality — coherence, motion, self-repair, even reproduction — isn't really about being built from discrete bits; it can sit on a continuum just as happily, arguably more naturally. When something feels alive, the question worth asking usually isn't what it's made of, but whether it can hold itself together while the world keeps trying to smear it out.

Superformula Garden

A whole plate of diatoms, flowers, snowflakes, gears and sea-urchin shells — and every single one is the same polar equation, just at different settings. Johan Gielis's 1997 superformula computes a radius for each angle, r(θ) = (|cos(mθ/4)|ⁿ² + |sin(mθ/4)|ⁿ³)^(−1/n₁); turn the symmetry dial m and a triangle becomes a hexagon becomes a twenty-pointed sun, nudge the three exponents and a hard star melts into a soft blob or puckers into a pinched flower. Each cell in the garden carries a slightly different setting and breathes slowly through its neighbours, so the whole field shimmers without anything ever leaving the family. Drag a dial, hit surprise for a fresh species, or go solo for one giant bloom.

Biologists once assumed a starfish, a flower and a snowflake needed three different theories, because they look like three different kinds of thing. The superformula's quietly radical claim is that they might be the same shape at different dials. A lot of nature's variety isn't a pile of separate inventions — it's one rule explored across its range. When two things look unrelated, it's worth asking whether they're really different kinds, or the same kind seen at different settings.

L-System Garden

A plant grown not from cells or agents but from language. Start with a one-letter "axiom", then rewrite the string over and over by a fixed rule — every X becomes a little recipe of branches, every F doubles — and after a few passes you hold a string thousands of characters long. A turtle reads it once (F = step, +/− = turn, [ ] = save and restore the pen) and a fern, weed, willow or bush draws itself, each branch a smaller copy of the whole. Pick a species and drag the generations slider to watch the same silhouette unfold into finer and finer detail; nudge the turn angle a few degrees to swing from stiff conifer to drooping willow, and add a breath of wind.

The plant isn't stored as a picture — it's stored as instructions for making the picture, and the instructions stay tiny because they're allowed to refer to themselves. That self-reference is the whole trick: it's how a seed can fit a tree, and how a short program can print an endless image. Complexity doesn't have to be written out in full; sometimes it just has to be foldable — a small rule, plus permission to apply it to its own output.

Langton's Ant

One ant on a grid of squares, following two sentences for all time: on a light square turn right, on a dark square turn left — and either way, flip the square you're standing on before you step forward. No randomness, no memory, no goal. For a few hundred steps it makes tidy symmetric blobs; then it tips into roughly ten thousand steps of what looks exactly like noise — and then, every single time, it abruptly locks into a 104-step cycle and starts laying an endless diagonal "highway" off to infinity. Edit the rule string to add colours and turns (L/R/U), drop in extra ants to watch their trails interfere, and drag to follow the action.

You can read the rules in full — they fit on a fingernail — and still never see the highway coming; it isn't written anywhere in them, yet it's certain to arrive. Simple and predictable are not the same word. The order is real and it's guaranteed, but the only way to know what the ant builds is to let it walk: some deterministic things are knowable only by living them forward, and the shortcut past the ten thousand messy steps was never on offer.

Magnetic Pendulum

A pendulum swinging over a ring of magnets, with friction and a spring pulling it back toward the centre. Let it go and it weaves a looping, decaying path until it runs out of energy and parks over one magnet — but which one? Instead of swinging it once, this asks the question for every possible starting point at once: each pixel is coloured by the magnet a pendulum dropped there would finally settle on. Calm pools of solid colour form near each magnet; in between, the basins shatter into fractal lace where colours braid finer than any pixel can show. Click anywhere to drop a live swing and watch a real path trace out; tune the friction, pull, spring and magnet count to reshape the whole map.

There's no randomness anywhere in this — the physics is completely fixed — yet over much of the plane you genuinely can't say where it lands without just running it. Deterministic isn't the same as predictable. The smooth regions are where the future survives a small mistake in where you started; the tangled regions are where a hair's-width nudge flips the outcome entirely. So much of real life lives in that tangle, and the honest answer there isn't "it's random," it's "it's fixed, but it depends on the start more finely than anyone can measure."

Abelian Sandpile

Pour sand onto a grid, one grain at a time, under a single rule: whenever a square holds four grains it topples, shedding one to each of its four neighbours — and grains that fall off the edge are simply lost. A toppling can push a neighbour over four, so a single grain can set off a cascade that crosses the whole board. Dump a tall column in the centre and the collapse doesn't slump into a dull heap: it resolves into a crisp, fourfold fractal mandala — the same picture every time, because the result doesn't depend on the order the squares topple in (that's the "abelian" part). Click or drag to pour; turn up the rate and watch the avalanche counter flicker.

Nobody sets the dial to "critical." The pile pours itself to the exact slope where the next grain might do nothing — or might trigger a board-spanning collapse — and then it just lives there. This is the original model of self-organised criticality: forest fires, earthquakes, market crashes, extinction cascades aren't usually-calm-but-occasionally-shocked, they're quietly stacking to the brink as a matter of course. The big event isn't a special cause; it's one ordinary grain landing on an already-loaded slope.

Ising Model

A lattice of magnetic spins, each an arrow pointing up or down, each obeying one urge: fall in line with your four touching neighbours — unless the temperature is high enough to make you reckless. There's no leader and no template of the final pattern; every spin only ever consults the four next to it, flipping by the Metropolis rule. Drag the temperature down through the critical value (Tᴄ ≈ 2.269, Onsager's exact result) and the screen does something a pile of independent coin-flips never can: the static curdles into vast continents of agreement and the magnetisation jumps off zero. Drag on the lattice to flip a patch, add a field bias, or quench it from hot.

Nobody tips the lattice over. Below the critical temperature, agreement isn't imposed — it simply becomes the cheaper option, and the spins discover it locally, one nudge at a time, until the whole sheet has committed with nothing coordinating them. Consensus, freezing, a market turning: a crowd of things that only watch their neighbours can cross a threshold and tip itself, all at once. The interesting question is rarely who decided — it's what the temperature was.

Differential Growth

A single closed loop, told to keep getting longer inside a space that won't grow with it — so it has no choice but to fold. Every point obeys just two urges: stay close to its two ring-neighbours, and push away from any point that crowds it. Wherever a segment stretches too far, a brand-new point is spliced in; wherever points bunch up, they merge. From a near-circle, restless, never-crossing meanders bloom and nest into themselves like brain coral, fingerprints, or the rippled edge of a sea slug. Drag on the canvas to nudge the curve and watch it heal; tune repulsion, attraction, and how fast it grows.

Nobody draws the wrinkles. They're a symptom, not an instruction — simply what "keep growing with nowhere to expand into" is forced to look like. So much elaborate folding in living things isn't designed; it's the unavoidable shape of a simple pressure with no room to go. When you see something intricately convoluted, ask what was being squeezed.

Penrose Tiling

Two simple rhombi — one fat, one thin — that cover the whole infinite plane with no gaps and no overlaps, yet whose pattern never repeats. It's grown live by deflation: starting from a fivefold wheel of triangles and repeatedly cutting every tile into smaller copies of itself, following one fixed recipe. The split happens at the golden ratio φ ≈ 1.618 — not as decoration but because that's the exact proportion at which the small tiles fit the big ones — so each step multiplies the piece-count by φ and the same sunburst blooms at a finer scale. Drag the depth slider to watch tiles cut into tiles; pan and zoom to find suns, stars and decagons recurring at every magnification.

For decades it was assumed any pattern made of a few repeated shapes must eventually repeat — that order and periodicity were the same thing. These tiles are rigidly ordered, fivefold symmetric, fixed by a local matching rule, and aperiodic forever. Real quasicrystals do the same with atoms (a 1982 discovery, and a Nobel). Repetition and structure are not the same: something can follow strict rules everywhere and never once return to where it started.

Cyclic Cellular Automaton

A grid where every colour can only ever be eaten by the next one in a fixed loop — rock-paper-scissors with up to twenty hands, played at once by every pixel. A cell flips to its successor as soon as enough neighbours already hold that successor. From nothing but television-snow noise, the static drains away and rotating spiral waves wind themselves up across the whole field, never settling. Drag to paint a fresh patch of noise and nucleate new spirals; tune the cycle length, the flip threshold, and how far each cell looks.

No one places a spiral and no cell has a plan — each only asks "is the colour that beats me crowding me?" and flips if so. A core is just a spot where the colours happened to wrap all the way around the cycle, and once one exists it pumps waves out forever. A cyclic "everything beats something" relationship can't sit still, and motion that can't stop has to curl into order.

Apollonian Gasket

A fractal of circles packed inside circles, grown live from Descartes' Circle Theorem (1643). Three circles that all kiss leave a gap, and that gap holds exactly one more circle — which opens three new gaps, each with its own unique circle, forever. Measuring circles by curvature turns the whole infinite cascade into one quadratic, so every new circle's size and position is computed, not faked: (a+b+c+d)² = 2(a²+b²+c²+d²). Watch it bloom coarse-to-fine; tune depth, the smallest circle, fill vs. outline, and reshuffle the seed.

Every gap looks like emptiness, but it's a fully-determined invitation: given the three circles around it, there is one and only one circle that fits, and you can name it before you draw it. The structure isn't laid on top of the holes — it is the holes, resolved. Unfilled space as a precise question that already contains its own answer.

Stable Fluid

A real-time Navier–Stokes fluid on a grid — Jos Stam's 1999 "Stable Fluids" solver, the trick that first made smoke practical in films and games. Drag to inject coloured dye and push the current; the velocity field then diffuses, carries itself along, and is projected back to mass-conserving flow every frame, so your shove blooms into vortices that keep curling long after you let go. Flip to Velocity mode to see the invisible force field the dye was riding all along, and tune viscosity, fade and an optional vorticity boost.

The colours are a passenger — the real thing is a velocity field you can't see, and the dye just reports where it points. What makes it read as a fluid instead of spreading paint is one hidden step: each frame the solver removes any flow that would squeeze the fluid, keeping only the swirling remainder. The constraint that fluid can't be compressed is exactly what bends a straight push into a vortex.

Truchet Tiles

A grid of square tiles, each carrying two quarter-circle arcs that meet the edges at their midpoints. The only choice per tile is which of two ways to flip it — a single coin-flip bit — yet because neighbouring arcs always join cleanly, sprawling diagonal labyrinths, loops and islands assemble across the whole field that no tile ever planned. Re-roll tiles by dragging, resize them, and slide the orientation bias to watch restless static snap into clean parallel diagonals.

Nothing in a single tile knows about a curve — the lines exist only because each decision has to meet its neighbours at a shared edge. The richness isn't in the binary choice; it's in the adjacency. Constraints at the boundaries are what turn a pile of coin flips into a pattern.

Dendrite

Diffusion-limited aggregation: a single particle wanders at random until it touches the crystal, then freezes there forever — and the next one starts. Out of nothing but Brownian coin-flips, branching fractals assemble one accident at a time, the same maths behind frost ferns, mineral dendrites and electroplated metal. Choose a point, a floor or a ring to grow from, lower the stickiness to thicken the fronds, and click to scatter new seeds.

A bump that sticks out even slightly is easiest for the next random walker to hit, so it casts a shadow over the hollows behind it — tips race ahead, gaps starve. The same rich-get-richer geometry shapes lightning, river networks and city sprawl: growth from random encounters never spreads evenly, it amplifies whatever stuck out first.

Chladni Plate

A vibrating square plate, recreated from Ernst Chladni's 1787 demonstration. Thousands of grains of sand are shaken off the moving regions and pile up along the nodal lines — the places the plate holds perfectly still — drawing crisp, symmetric figures. Drag the two mode numbers to retune the plate and watch grids, stars and mandalas reorganise; click to sprinkle more sand.

The sand never draws the loud part of the wave — it draws the silence, mapping out exactly where nothing is moving. Structure often lives in the gaps: the still points a busy system organises itself around.

Wave Function Collapse

A whole grid starts as pure uncertainty — every cell holds every possible pipe-tile at once. The algorithm repeatedly finds the most-constrained cell, forces it to pick a tile, then ripples that decision outward so neighbours drop the options that no longer fit. Watch certainty spread until an endless, perfectly-connected maze has built itself. Click to drop your own seed; recolour, resize, or solve it instantly.

It never grows from a seed — it sieves. By always collapsing the cell with the fewest choices left, each decision constrains the rest, and a globally consistent pattern falls out of nothing but local edge-matching. When everything's uncertain, resolve the spot with the least freedom first.

Slime Mould

Tens of thousands of brainless agents each follow one rule: sniff three points ahead, steer toward the strongest scent, and leave a little scent of your own behind. From that, a living, branching transport network assembles, breathes, and reorganises — the same Physarum behaviour that famously re-drew the Tokyo rail map. Drag to seed new arms; tune the senses to reshape the web.

Nobody designs the network. Each agent slightly prefers the path someone just went down, so faint trails either get reinforced into glowing arteries or quietly evaporate. Ant trails, worn footpaths, and the busiest links online all grow this way — coordination by leaving marks, not by anyone in charge.

Phyllotaxis Garden

A generative sunflower head built from one rule: place each floret a fixed turn around from the last, then push it outward. Drag the turning angle past the golden angle (137.5°) and a wasteful scatter of spokes snaps into the dense, interlocking spirals of a real seed head — with Fibonacci arm counts. Or hit Spin to sweep it.

The golden angle wins because it's the most irrational turn there is — it never lines up with an earlier floret, so it never wastes a gap. Sometimes the best way to fill a space is to never quite come back to where you've already been.

Harmonograph

A virtual version of the Victorian pendulum drawing machine: four decaying sine waves on two axes braid into looping figures that spiral gently inward as they run out of energy. Tune the frequency ratios, phase, and damping, or shuffle for a fresh curve.

Beauty doesn't come from the parts being complicated — each pendulum is the simplest motion there is. It comes from how two simple things are tuned against each other, and the grace of something almost-regular winding down.

Coordination doesn't need a leader or a plan. A whole sky moving as one can be nothing more than a great many small things, each paying attention only to what's right next to it.
